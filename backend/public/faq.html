Frequently Asked Questions – Financial Planning

1. How should I start planning my finances?

Start by setting clear financial goals, tracking your income and expenses, creating a monthly budget, and building an emergency fund.

2. How much should I save each month?

A common rule is the 50/30/20 rule: 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and 20% for savings and debt repayment.

3. Do I need an emergency fund, and how much should it be?

Yes, aim for 3–6 months’ worth of living expenses to cover unexpected situations like job loss or medical emergencies.

4. What’s the best way to get out of debt?

Use the debt snowball (smallest debts first) or debt avalanche (highest interest first) method, and avoid new debt.

5. When should I start investing, and where?

Start as early as possible. Begin with diversified options like mutual funds, SIPs, or index funds, based on your risk profile.

6. What’s the difference between saving and investing?

Saving is short-term and low-risk (e.g., savings accounts), while investing is for long-term growth (e.g., stocks, real estate).

7. How can I plan for retirement?

Start early by contributing to retirement plans like NPS, PPF, or EPF, and calculate your future needs based on your desired lifestyle.

8. Should I buy insurance, and which types are necessary?

Yes. Basic types include term life insurance and health insurance. Consider disability and critical illness cover if needed.

Student FAQs – Financial Planning

1. How can I start saving money as a student with limited income?

Track your expenses, save a small fixed amount from your allowance or part-time income, and take advantage of student discounts and scholarships.

2. Should I invest while I'm still a student?

If you have some regular income and no urgent debt, consider starting with low-risk investments like SIPs or RDs to benefit from compounding early on.

3. How can I manage my finances if I don’t earn any income yet?

Set a monthly budget for your pocket money, track spending, and focus on building good financial habits like saving and distinguishing between needs and wants.
